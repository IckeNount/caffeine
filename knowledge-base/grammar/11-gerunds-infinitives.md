# Gerunds & Infinitives: V+V Patterns in a Serial Verb Language

## Academic Overview
English verb complementation — whether a verb is followed by a gerund (V-ing), an infinitive (to + V), or either — is one of the most lexically determined and difficult-to-systematize areas of English grammar. There is no single rule that predicts whether a verb takes a gerund or infinitive; it must largely be memorized on a verb-by-verb basis (e.g., "enjoy + V-ing" but "want + to V").

Thai, as a serial verb language (SVO with verb chaining), handles verb-after-verb sequences without any morphological marking. Thai simply chains bare verbs: ฉัน**ชอบ กิน** ส้ม (I + like + eat + orange). There is no equivalent of "-ing" or "to" between verbs. This means Thai learners must acquire an entirely new system of verb complementation that has no L1 parallel.

The challenge is compounded by cases where the choice between gerund and infinitive changes meaning: "I stopped **smoking**" (quit the habit) vs. "I stopped **to smoke**" (paused in order to smoke). These semantic distinctions encoded through morphosyntax have no counterpart in Thai.

## English vs. Thai: Key Differences

### Thai Serial Verb Construction
Thai simply chains verbs together in sequence without any linking particle:

- ฉัน**อยาก กิน** ข้าว (I + want + eat + rice) — No "to" needed
- ฉัน**ชอบ ว่ายน้ำ** (I + like + swim) — No "-ing" needed
- เขา**เริ่ม ทำ**งาน (He + start + do + work) — No gerund/infinitive choice
- ฉัน**หยุด พูด** (I + stop + speak) — Ambiguous without context; Thai has no morphological way to distinguish "stopped speaking" from "stopped to speak"

### Why This Causes Persistent Errors
1. Thai students see no reason for "to" or "-ing" — the verb chain works fine bare
2. They cannot predict which form to use because there is no equivalent rule in Thai
3. The meaning-change pairs (stop, remember, try, forget) require abstract grammatical reasoning that Thai doesn't encode

## Chunking Rules for LinguBreak

### Gerund/Infinitive as Verb Complement
When a gerund or infinitive follows another verb as its complement, they form part of a larger verb-object relationship:

1. **Main verb + gerund (as object)**: The gerund phrase is the `object` chunk
   - "She enjoys **reading books**" → verb: "enjoys" + object: "reading books"
   - The gerund acts as the thing being enjoyed

2. **Main verb + infinitive (as object)**: The infinitive phrase is the `object` chunk
   - "She wants **to read books**" → verb: "wants" + object: "to read books"

3. **Gerund as subject**: The gerund phrase is the `subject` chunk
   - "**Swimming** is fun" → subject: "Swimming" + verb: "is" + modifier: "fun"
   - "**Reading books** makes you smarter" → subject: "Reading books"

4. **Infinitive of purpose**: Label as `modifier`
   - "She went to the library **to study**" → modifier: "to study"
   - This is purpose/reason, not a verb complement

### Critical Rule
Do NOT split "to" from its infinitive verb. "to study" = one unit. Similarly, do NOT split a gerund from its object: "reading books" is one `object` chunk when functioning as a verb complement.

## Thai Logic Reconstruction

### Reordering for Gerunds/Infinitives
Thai verb chaining keeps the same SVO order, so reordering is minimal:

1. **Verb + gerund**: Keep verb chain in same order
   - "She enjoys reading books" → Thai: เธอ + ชอบ + อ่านหนังสือ (She + enjoy + read books)
   - Note: Drop the "-ing" concept entirely in Thai reconstruction

2. **Verb + infinitive**: Keep verb chain in same order
   - "She wants to eat rice" → Thai: เธอ + อยาก + กินข้าว (She + want + eat rice)
   - Note: Drop the "to" concept entirely

3. **Gerund as subject**: Optionally add การ (gaan) prefix for formality
   - "Swimming is good exercise" → Thai: การว่ายน้ำ + เป็น + การออกกำลังกายที่ดี
   - การ functions as a nominalizer, similar to English "-ing" as a noun

4. **Infinitive of purpose**: Use เพื่อ (phûea = in order to) or ไป/มา
   - "I went to the store to buy milk" → Thai: ฉัน + ไปร้าน + เพื่อซื้อนม

## Common L1 Interference Errors (Thai Students)

1. **Using infinitive after gerund-only verbs**: "I enjoy **to swim**" → "I enjoy **swimming**"
   - *Cause*: Thai ชอบว่ายน้ำ has no morphological marker, so both forms feel interchangeable.

2. **Using gerund after infinitive-only verbs**: "I want **eating**" → "I want **to eat**"
   - *Cause*: Same reason — Thai อยากกิน gives no clue about which English form to use.

3. **Omitting "to" in infinitives**: "I decided **∅ go**" → "I decided **to go**"
   - *Cause*: Thai ตัดสินใจไป chains verbs directly without any linking word.

4. **Confusing meaning-change pairs**: "I remembered **to lock** the door" (= I didn't forget) vs. "I remembered **locking** the door" (= I recall the past action)
   - *Cause*: Thai ฉันจำได้ว่าล็อคประตู is identical for both meanings; context disambiguates.

5. **Using bare verb after "make/let/help"**: This is actually correct! "She **made** me **go**" (bare infinitive after causative). Thai students get this one right by accident because Thai also chains bare verbs.

## Golden Examples

1. **Gerund as object**:
   - "The students avoided **making the same mistakes** on the final exam."
   - Core SVO: "The students avoided making mistakes"
   - Thai: นักเรียนหลีกเลี่ยงการทำผิดซ้ำในข้อสอบปลายภาค

2. **Infinitive as object**:
   - "She decided **to study abroad** after graduating from university."
   - Core SVO: "She decided to study abroad"
   - Thai: เธอตัดสินใจไปเรียนต่างประเทศหลังจากจบมหาวิทยาลัย

3. **Meaning-change pair (stop)**:
   - "He stopped **smoking** last year." (quit the habit)
   - "He stopped **to smoke** outside the building." (paused to go smoke)
   - Thai: เขาเลิกสูบบุหรี่เมื่อปีที่แล้ว / เขาหยุดเพื่อไปสูบบุหรี่นอกตึก

4. **Gerund as subject**:
   - "**Learning a second language** requires patience and daily practice."
   - Core SVO: "Learning requires patience"
   - Thai: การเรียนภาษาที่สองต้องใช้ความอดทนและการฝึกฝนทุกวัน
