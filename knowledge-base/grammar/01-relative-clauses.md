# Relative Clauses: who, which, that → ที่ (thîi)

## Overview
Relative clauses are the MOST important grammar pattern for Thai students to master when reading English. In English, relative clauses are introduced by "who", "which", "that", "whose", "whom", and "where". In Thai, ALL of these map to a single word: **ที่** (thîi).

## Key Differences

### English: Relative clause INTERRUPTS the main sentence
- "The student **who studied hard** passed the exam."
- The main sentence is: "The student passed the exam."
- The relative clause "who studied hard" is inserted between the subject and the verb.

### Thai: Modifier comes AFTER the noun, connected by ที่
- นักเรียน**ที่เรียนหนัก** สอบผ่าน
- (Student + ที่ + studied hard + passed exam)
- The modifier follows the noun naturally.

## Pattern Mapping

| English | Thai Structure |
|---------|---------------|
| The man **who** works here | ผู้ชาย**ที่**ทำงานที่นี่ |
| The book **that/which** I read | หนังสือ**ที่**ฉันอ่าน |
| The place **where** I live | สถานที่**ที่**ฉันอยู่ |
| The person **whose** car broke | คน**ที่**รถเสีย |

## Common Patterns in Complex Sentences

### Nested Relative Clauses
English: "The teacher who taught the student who failed was upset."
Breaking this down:
1. Core SVO: "The teacher was upset"
2. First ที่: "teacher who taught the student" → ครูที่สอนนักเรียน
3. Second ที่: "student who failed" → นักเรียนที่สอบตก

### Non-restrictive (extra info) vs Restrictive (essential)
English uses commas to distinguish:
- "My brother, who lives in Bangkok, is a doctor." (extra info — I have one brother)
- "My brother who lives in Bangkok is a doctor." (essential — I have multiple brothers)

Thai doesn't make this distinction with punctuation. Both use ที่.

## Chunking Rules for Relative Clauses
- The relative pronoun (who/which/that) and everything up to the next main verb or punctuation = ONE chunk
- Label: `relative_clause`
- Color: Green (bg-green-100)
- The word "ที่" should always appear in the Thai explanation

## Common Thai Student Mistakes
1. **Omitting the relative pronoun**: Thai doesn't always need ที่ for short modifiers, so students may drop "who/which/that" in English.
2. **Wrong word order**: Putting the relative clause before the noun (Thai influence).
3. **Using "which" for people**: Thai ที่ works for everything, so students don't distinguish who vs which.
