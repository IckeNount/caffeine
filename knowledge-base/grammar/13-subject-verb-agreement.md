# Subject-Verb Agreement: Inflection in a Non-Inflecting Language

## Academic Overview
Subject-verb agreement (concord) in English requires that finite verbs agree with their subjects in person and number. In practice, this is most visible in the present simple tense, where third-person singular subjects require the "-s/-es" suffix on the verb (e.g., "She **speaks**" vs. "They **speak**"), and in the selection of "be" forms (am/is/are/was/were). English agreement is relatively simple compared to highly inflected languages like Spanish or Arabic, but it is nonetheless a significant acquisition challenge for Thai learners.

Thai is a zero-agreement language: verbs never change form based on the subject. The verb กิน (gin = eat) is identical whether the subject is first person singular, third person singular, or third person plural. There is no morphological marking whatsoever — ฉันกิน, เขากิน, พวกเขากิน all use the identical verb form.

This means that Thai students must learn to monitor subject-verb agreement as a conscious grammatical process, checking the subject's person and number before selecting the verb form. This monitoring process is cognitively demanding and produces errors that persist even at advanced levels (CEFR B2–C1), particularly in complex sentences where the subject and verb are separated by intervening phrases.

CEFR suitability: Basic 3rd person -s (A2), Agreement with complex subjects (B1–B2), Agreement with quantifiers and collective nouns (B2–C1).

## English vs. Thai: Key Differences

### Thai Zero-Agreement System
Thai verbs are completely invariable:

| Subject | English Verb | Thai Verb |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| I | speak | พูด |
| You | speak | พูด |
| He/She | speak**s** | พูด |
| We | speak | พูด |
| They | speak | พูด |

The concept of adding "-s" to a verb because the subject is "he" instead of "I" is entirely foreign to Thai grammar. There is no L1 rule that even approximates this behavior.

### Additional Agreement Complexity
Beyond the basic -s rule, English agreement extends to:
- **Be-verbs**: am/is/are (present), was/were (past)
- **Have**: have/has (present)
- **Do**: do/does (questions/negation)
- **Collective nouns**: "The team is/are..."
- **Quantifiers**: "Each/every + singular" vs. "All/both + plural"
- **"There is/are"** constructions: Agreement with the *following* noun

## Chunking Rules for LinguBreak

### Agreement Within Chunks
Subject-verb agreement does NOT affect chunk boundaries. The subject and verb remain in their own separate chunks as usual:

1. **Subject chunk**: Includes the full noun phrase, regardless of number
   - "**The students**" → subject (plural triggers "are/have/verb-without-s")
   - "**Each student**" → subject (singular despite referring to many)

2. **Verb chunk**: Includes the correctly inflected verb
   - "The students **are studying**" → verb: "are studying"
   - "Each student **is studying**" → verb: "is studying"

3. **Intervening prepositional phrases**: These are their OWN chunk and do NOT change the agreement
   - "The student **in the advanced classes** studies hard" → subject: "The student" + prepositional: "in the advanced classes" + verb: "studies"
   - Agreement is with "student" (singular), NOT "classes"

### Thai Explanation Guidance
In the `thai_explanation`, highlight that:
- English verbs change form to match the subject — Thai verbs never change
- The "-s" on "speaks" means the subject is 3rd person singular
- "are" means the subject is plural, "is" means singular

## Thai Logic Reconstruction

### Agreement in Thai Reconstruction
Since Thai has no agreement system, the reconstruction drops all agreement markers:

1. **-s on verbs**: Disappears
   - "She speaks English" → Thai: เธอ + พูดภาษาอังกฤษ (just เธอ + พูด, no -s equivalent)

2. **am/is/are distinctions**: Collapse to single Thai form
   - "I am / She is / They are happy" → All become: [subject] + มีความสุข
   - Thai เป็น or คือ doesn't change based on subject

3. **has/have distinction**: Collapse to มี
   - "She has / They have a book" → Both: [subject] + มีหนังสือ

### Key Point for Reconstruction
Agreement is an English-only phenomenon. In the Thai reordered version, simply use the base verb form for all subjects. The `thai_explanation` should explicitly note: "ในภาษาไทย กริยาไม่เปลี่ยนรูปตามประธาน" (In Thai, verbs don't change form based on the subject).

## Common L1 Interference Errors (Thai Students)

1. **Dropping 3rd person -s**: "She **speak** English well" → "She **speaks** English well"
   - *Cause*: Thai เธอพูดภาษาอังกฤษดี — the verb พูด never changes regardless of subject.

2. **Wrong "be" form**: "They **is** my friends" → "They **are** my friends"
   - *Cause*: Thai เป็น is used for all subjects without change. พวกเขาเป็นเพื่อนของฉัน.

3. **Agreement with intervening phrase**: "The quality of **the products are** good" → "The quality of the products **is** good"
   - *Cause*: Students match the verb to the nearest noun ("products") instead of the true subject ("quality"). Thai has no agreement rule to guide them.

4. **There is/are confusion**: "There **is** many students" → "There **are** many students"
   - *Cause*: Thai มีนักเรียนหลายคน uses one form of มี regardless of whether what follows is singular or plural.

5. **Overcorrection with -s**: "They **speaks** English" → "They **speak** English"
   - *Cause*: Once students learn the -s rule, they sometimes apply it universally, including to plural subjects.

## Golden Examples

1. **Basic 3rd person agreement**:
   - "My brother **works** at a hospital that **is** near our house."
   - Core SVO: "My brother works at a hospital"
   - Thai: พี่ชายของฉันทำงานที่โรงพยาบาลที่อยู่ใกล้บ้าน

2. **Agreement with intervening phrase**:
   - "The results of the experiment **show** that the hypothesis **was** correct."
   - Core SVO: "The results show that the hypothesis was correct"
   - Thai: ผลการทดลองแสดงว่าสมมติฐานถูกต้อง

3. **There is/are construction**:
   - "There **are** several reasons why the student who **was** absent **needs** to take the make-up exam."
   - Core SVO: "There are reasons" + "the student needs to take the exam"
   - Thai: มีหลายเหตุผลว่าทำไมนักเรียนที่ขาดเรียนต้องสอบซ่อม

4. **Complex subject with quantifier**:
   - "Every student in the advanced classes **has** submitted the assignment that **was** due last Friday."
   - Core SVO: "Every student has submitted the assignment"
   - Thai: นักเรียนทุกคนในชั้นเรียนขั้นสูงส่งงานที่ครบกำหนดเมื่อวันศุกร์ที่แล้วแล้ว
