# Thai Logic Reconstruction: How to Reorder English for Thai Readers

## Core Principle
Thai and English share SVO (Subject-Verb-Object) order, but differ fundamentally in WHERE modifiers are placed. The goal of "Thai Logic" reconstruction is to rearrange English chunks so a Thai reader can process them in their natural reading order.

## The Fundamental Rule
**English**: Modifier → Head word (adjective before noun, relative clause after)
**Thai**: Head word → Modifier (everything comes after what it modifies)

## Step-by-Step Reconstruction Process

### Step 1: Identify Core SVO
Strip everything to find the skeleton:
- "The **tall** student **who studied hard** passed the **difficult** exam **that was given by the professor** **last week**."
- Core: "The student passed the exam"

### Step 2: Reattach Modifiers After Their Nouns
English: "The **tall** student" → Thai logic: "student **tall**" (นักเรียนตัวสูง)
English: "the **difficult** exam" → Thai logic: "exam **difficult**" (ข้อสอบยาก)

### Step 3: Attach Relative Clauses with ที่
English: "student **who studied hard**" → Thai: "student **ที่เรียนหนัก**"
The relative clause follows the noun it modifies.

### Step 4: Place Time/Place at the End (or Beginning)
English time phrases can go anywhere. Thai usually places them:
- At the beginning (topic position): "เมื่อสัปดาห์ที่แล้ว นักเรียน..."
- At the end: "...เมื่อสัปดาห์ที่แล้ว"

## Reordering Examples

### Example 1: Simple Modifier Reorder
**English chunks**: [The beautiful] [house] [on the hill] [is] [very old]
**Thai logic**: [house] [beautiful] [on the hill] [is] [old] [very]
**Thai**: บ้านสวยบนเนินเขาเก่ามาก

### Example 2: Relative Clause
**English chunks**: [The man] [who works here] [is] [my father]
**Thai logic**: [man] [ที่ works here] [is] [father] [my]
**Thai**: ผู้ชายที่ทำงานที่นี่เป็นพ่อของฉัน

### Example 3: Complex Sentence
**English**: "The book that I borrowed from the library which was built in 1920 is very interesting."
**English chunks**: [The book] [that I borrowed] [from the library] [which was built in 1920] [is] [very interesting]
**Thai logic**: [book] [ที่ I borrowed] [from library] [ที่ was built in 1920] [interesting] [very]
**Thai**: หนังสือที่ฉันยืมจากห้องสมุดที่สร้างเมื่อปี 1920 น่าสนใจมาก

### Example 4: Passive to Active
**English**: "The exam was given by the professor to the students in the morning."
**Thai logic**: [professor] [gave] [exam] [to students] [in the morning]
**Thai**: อาจารย์ให้ข้อสอบนักเรียนตอนเช้า
Note: Passive → Active is often more natural in Thai

## Reordering Rules Summary

| English Position | Thai Position | Example |
|-----------------|--------------|---------|
| Adj + Noun | Noun + Adj | big house → house big (บ้านใหญ่) |
| Adv + Verb | Verb + Adv | quickly ran → ran quickly (วิ่งเร็ว) |
| N + Relative Clause | N + ที่ + Clause | same position! (but add ที่) |
| Prep phrase | After verb/noun | same position, usually |
| Possessive + Noun | Noun + ของ + Owner | my book → book ของ I (หนังสือของฉัน) |
| Aux + Main Verb | Same order | has eaten → has eaten (equivalent) |

## When NOT to Reorder
Some structures are similar enough:
1. Basic SVO: "I eat rice" = ฉันกินข้าว — same order
2. Question words at start: similar in formal Thai
3. Prepositional phrases after verbs: often same position

## Quality Checklist for Reconstruction
- [ ] Every chunk from the English version appears in the Thai version
- [ ] No chunks are duplicated or omitted
- [ ] Modifiers follow their head nouns
- [ ] Relative clauses have ที่ in the explanation
- [ ] The Thai translation sounds natural, not word-for-word
- [ ] Passive constructions are considered for active rewrite
